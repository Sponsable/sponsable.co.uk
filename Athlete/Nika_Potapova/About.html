Before there were walls, there were trees. That's where Nika learned to climb. When a friend took her to a gym, she was asked after a single session if she wanted to move straight into the advanced group.

She said yes.

Within her first year she climbed 8a. By thirteen, 8c. At fourteen she broke the women's Zlagboard endurance record — and nearly the men's. Not officially, but there's a video. At sixteen she climbed 9a, the 23rd woman in history to do so. That same year she became European Senior Champion in the combined discipline. A few months later, Youth World Champion.

Her coach controlled everything — who travelled, where they slept, who they spoke to. Athletes were turned against one another, competition engineered into isolation and justified by results. The situation eventually led to a court case in which Nika, seventeen, appeared as a witness.

"I was winning. But somebody was taking pieces from me."

She began shaping her own path. When the war began, she left Ukraine, training between Germany and Innsbruck, hitching rides to World Cups and sleeping wherever she could.

For now she is in Paris, with a room of her own, rediscovering that her best climbing happens when she stops thinking about it at all.

"I know I can climb much better than I am right now. I'll come back. Just wait for my comeback."
