Before there were walls, there were trees. That's where Nika learned to climb. When a friend took her to a gym, she was asked after a single session if she wanted to move straight into the advanced group.

She said yes.

Within her first year she climbed 8a. By thirteen, 8c. At fourteen she broke the women's Zlagboard endurance record — and nearly the men's. Not officially, but there's a video. At sixteen she climbed 9a, becoming the 23rd woman in history to do so. That same year she became European Youth Champion; a year later, Youth World Champion.

Her coach controlled everything — who travelled, where they slept, who they spoke to. Athletes were turned against one another, competition engineered into isolation and justified by results. He later became the subject of a court case concerning allegations of pedophilia. Nika, seventeen, was a witness.

"I was winning. But somebody was taking pieces from me."

She moved to Innsbruck in 2021, one of climbing's main training bases, hitching rides to World Cups, sleeping wherever she could. Now she's in Paris, with a room of her own, rediscovering that her best climbing happens when she stops thinking about it at all.

"I know I can climb much better than I am right now. I'll come back. Just wait for my comeback."

Ask Nika Answered monthly
What does a normal training day look like for you right now?
Morning session on the board, usually two hours. Afternoon I do something that isn't climbing — gym, run, just walk around Paris. I used to train until I couldn't move. I don't do that anymore.
Do you ever think about quitting?
Yes. And then I get on the wall and I remember why I started. It's not complicated. It just feels that way sometimes.
